CATEGORY 1: Getting Started & Equipment
1. Research and choose between binoculars or a telescope for your first astronomy equipment purchase.
2. Download a stargazing app like SkySafari, Stellarium, or Star Walk to your phone.
3. Find and join a local astronomy club or online community for beginners.
4. Create a dedicated astronomy journal or logbook for recording your observations.
5. Learn how to read a star chart and identify cardinal directions from your location.
6. Set up a comfortable observing spot in your backyard or identify nearby dark sky locations.
7. Purchase a red flashlight to preserve your night vision during observations.
8. Make or buy a simple planisphere (star wheel) for your latitude.
9. Learn how to properly clean and maintain optical equipment like telescope lenses.
10. Create a wishlist of astronomy gear organized by priority and budget.

CATEGORY 2: Learning the Night Sky
1. Memorize the major constellations visible in your current season.
2. Learn to find Polaris (North Star) using the Big Dipper as a guide.
3. Identify and track the phases of the Moon for one complete lunar cycle.
4. Locate and observe the five planets visible to the naked eye.
5. Learn the names and locations of the 20 brightest stars in the night sky.
6. Practice star-hopping techniques to find deep-sky objects without electronic aids.
7. Study the mythology and cultural stories behind major constellation names.
8. Create a personal observation checklist of must-see celestial objects.
9. Learn to identify different types of celestial objects by their appearance.
10. Master using celestial coordinates (right ascension and declination) to locate objects.

CATEGORY 3: Observation Planning & Tracking
1. Check weather forecasts and plan your next observation session for optimal conditions.
2. Research and mark upcoming celestial events like meteor showers and eclipses on your calendar.
3. Calculate the best viewing times for planets using astronomy software or apps.
4. Plan an observation session focused on one specific constellation or region of sky.
5. Create a monthly observation plan based on Moon phases and object visibility.
6. Track and record the International Space Station passes over your location.
7. Document seeing conditions using the Pickering or Antoniadi scales in your log.
8. Plan a trip to a dark sky site away from light pollution.
9. Organize your observations into a structured database or spreadsheet.
10. Set specific observation goals for each season of the year.

CATEGORY 4: Developing Skills & Techniques
1. Practice sketching the Moon's surface features and craters.
2. Learn to polar align an equatorial mount for better telescope tracking.
3. Master the technique of averted vision to see faint deep-sky objects.
4. Practice estimating stellar magnitudes by comparing star brightness.
5. Learn to collimate (align) your telescope's optics for sharper views.
6. Develop your ability to identify different types of nebulae by their appearance.
7. Practice measuring double star separations using eyepiece field of view.
8. Learn to recognize and identify artificial satellites versus natural objects.
9. Master the use of different eyepiece types and Barlow lenses for various targets.
10. Practice patience and develop longer attention spans for extended observations.

CATEGORY 5: Projects & Advanced Activities
1. Complete a Messier Marathon by observing all 110 Messier objects.
2. Start an astrophotography project using just your smartphone and telescope.
3. Contribute observations to citizen science projects like Globe at Night or AAVSO.
4. Build or modify a piece of astronomy equipment like a barn door tracker.
5. Create a detailed observing guide for your favorite constellation to share with others.
6. Document and report a fireball meteor sighting to the American Meteor Society.
7. Participate in an asteroid occultation timing observation.
8. Start a variable star observing program and submit magnitude estimates.
9. Organize or participate in a sidewalk astronomy event to share views with the public.
10. Design and execute a year-long project to photograph the Moon at the same phase monthly.
