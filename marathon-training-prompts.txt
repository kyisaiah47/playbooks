CATEGORY 1: Training Plan & Schedule
1. Create a 16-20 week training schedule with gradually increasing weekly mileage.
2. Schedule three to four running days per week with rest days in between.
3. Plan one long run per week, increasing distance by 10% each week.
4. Include one tempo run and one interval training session weekly.
5. Set specific daily distance goals for easy runs, tempo runs, and long runs.
6. Build in taper weeks every fourth week to allow recovery and adaptation.
7. Schedule cross-training activities like cycling or swimming on non-running days.
8. Plan race-pace practice runs at your target marathon pace.
9. Include hill training sessions to build strength and endurance.
10. Create backup indoor training options for bad weather days.

CATEGORY 2: Nutrition & Hydration
1. Calculate your daily caloric needs based on training volume and body weight.
2. Plan pre-run meals 2-3 hours before long training sessions.
3. Practice race-day breakfast during long run training sessions.
4. Test different sports drinks and energy gels during training runs.
5. Create a hydration schedule for runs longer than 60 minutes.
6. Plan post-run recovery meals with protein and carbohydrates within 30 minutes.
7. Increase carbohydrate intake during peak training weeks.
8. Practice fueling strategies for runs longer than 90 minutes.
9. Plan your race-day nutrition timeline from wake-up to finish line.
10. Stock up on familiar foods and avoid trying new things on race day.

CATEGORY 3: Injury Prevention & Recovery
1. Schedule dynamic warm-up routines before every run.
2. Plan 10-15 minutes of static stretching after each training session.
3. Include strength training exercises for runners twice per week.
4. Schedule weekly foam rolling or massage sessions.
5. Monitor daily sleep quality and aim for 7-9 hours per night.
6. Track any pain or discomfort in a training log.
7. Plan ice baths or cold therapy after long runs.
8. Schedule rest days when feeling overly fatigued or sore.
9. Include yoga or mobility work in your weekly routine.
10. Have a sports medicine professional on speed dial for injuries.

CATEGORY 4: Gear & Equipment
1. Get professionally fitted for running shoes at a specialty store.
2. Purchase 2-3 pairs of running shoes to rotate during training.
3. Test all race-day clothing during long training runs.
4. Buy moisture-wicking running clothes for different weather conditions.
5. Purchase a GPS watch or phone app to track pace and distance.
6. Stock up on blister prevention products like Body Glide or Vaseline.
7. Invest in proper running socks that prevent blisters and hot spots.
8. Buy a hydration belt or handheld water bottles for long runs.
9. Purchase reflective gear for early morning or evening runs.
10. Replace running shoes every 300-500 miles or when tread wears down.

CATEGORY 5: Mental Preparation & Race Strategy
1. Set realistic time goals based on your current fitness level.
2. Visualize race-day scenarios including challenging moments and your response.
3. Practice positive self-talk phrases to use during difficult training runs.
4. Research the race course elevation profile and plan pacing accordingly.
5. Create motivational playlists for different types of training runs.
6. Practice race-day logistics including parking, packet pickup, and timing.
7. Develop contingency plans for common race-day issues like bathroom stops.
8. Train your mind to break the race into smaller segments or milestones.
9. Study weather conditions and have clothing strategies for race day.
10. Plan your post-race celebration and recovery activities as motivation.
